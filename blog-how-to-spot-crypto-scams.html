The Scale of Crypto Scams in 2026

Cryptocurrency scams remain one of the biggest threats to crypto adoption and individual wealth. According to blockchain analytics firms, over $5.6 billion was lost to crypto scams and fraud in 2025 alone, and 2026 is on pace to exceed that number. As crypto becomes more mainstream, scammers are becoming more sophisticated, targeting everyone from first-time buyers to experienced DeFi users.

The good news: the vast majority of crypto scams follow recognizable patterns. If you know what to look for, you can avoid nearly all of them. This guide breaks down every major type of crypto scam, the warning signs to watch for, and concrete steps to protect your assets.

Warning: If someone contacts you unsolicited about a crypto "opportunity" promising guaranteed returns, it is almost certainly a scam. No legitimate investment guarantees profits. Full stop.

Rug Pulls: The Most Common DeFi Scam

A rug pull occurs when developers create a token, hype it up, attract investment, and then drain the liquidity pool, leaving investors with worthless tokens. Rug pulls accounted for over 35% of all crypto scam losses in 2025.

How Rug Pulls Work

The typical rug pull follows a predictable playbook:

  1. Token creation: Scammers deploy a new token on Ethereum, Solana, or Base with a catchy name, often riding trending memes or events
  2. Fake hype: They flood social media with paid promoters, fake engagement, and manufactured FOMO. Telegram and Discord groups show "organic" excitement
  3. Liquidity injection: Developers add initial liquidity to a DEX so people can buy the token
  4. Price pump: As more buyers enter, the price rises dramatically, creating more FOMO
  5. The rug: Developers remove all liquidity and/or dump their massive token allocation, crashing the price to near zero

How to Spot a Rug Pull

  • Unlocked liquidity: Check if liquidity pool tokens are locked using tools like DexScreener or RugCheck. If LP tokens are not locked, the developer can drain the pool at any time
  • Concentrated token holdings: If a small number of wallets hold 50%+ of the supply, it is a massive red flag. Use blockchain explorers to check holder distribution
  • Anonymous team: While many legitimate crypto projects have anonymous founders, anonymity combined with other red flags significantly increases risk
  • No audit: Legitimate projects get smart contract audits from firms like CertiK, Trail of Bits, or OpenZeppelin. No audit means no verification that the code does what it claims
  • Disabled selling: Some scam contracts include hidden functions that prevent token holders from selling (honeypot). Always test with a small amount first

Phishing Attacks and Wallet Drainers

Phishing remains the most direct attack vector against crypto users. Scammers create fake websites, apps, and communications designed to trick you into revealing your private keys, seed phrases, or approving malicious smart contract interactions.

Common Phishing Vectors

  • Fake websites: Pixel-perfect clones of popular platforms (Uniswap, MetaMask, Phantom) with slightly misspelled URLs. Always verify the URL character by character
  • Malicious wallet connect: Websites that prompt you to connect your wallet and then request unlimited token approvals, allowing them to drain your assets later
  • Fake airdrops: "Claim your free tokens" messages that lead to wallet drainer sites. If you did not expect an airdrop, it is almost certainly a scam
  • Support impersonation: Scammers posing as MetaMask support, Coinbase help, or exchange representatives in Discord, Telegram, and X DMs. No legitimate support team will ever ask for your seed phrase
  • Fake browser extensions: Malicious Chrome or Firefox extensions that mimic popular wallet extensions but intercept your transactions

Critical Rule: NEVER share your seed phrase or private keys with anyone, for any reason. No legitimate service, support team, or airdrop will ever require your seed phrase. If anyone asks for it, they are trying to steal your crypto.

Fake Exchanges and Trading Platforms

Fake crypto exchanges are sophisticated operations designed to look like legitimate trading platforms. They often feature professional-looking interfaces, fake trading volumes, and fabricated user testimonials. These scams typically operate by:

  • Allowing initial deposits and even small withdrawals to build trust
  • Showing fabricated trading profits on your "account"
  • Requesting increasingly larger deposits for "premium features" or to "unlock withdrawals"
  • Eventually blocking all withdrawal attempts and disappearing

How to Verify an Exchange

  • Check if the exchange is registered with relevant regulators (FinCEN in the U.S., FCA in the UK)
  • Search for independent reviews on trusted crypto media (CoinDesk, The Block, CoinGecko)
  • Verify the domain age using WHOIS lookup. Legitimate exchanges have years of history
  • Check scam.horse and our sister sites for scam reports on the platform
  • Stick to well-known exchanges: Coinbase, Kraken, Binance, and Gemini have proven track records

Ponzi Schemes and Yield Scams

Crypto Ponzi schemes promise impossibly high returns, typically framed as "yield farming," "staking rewards," or "trading bot profits." They pay early investors with funds from new investors, creating the illusion of legitimate returns until the scheme inevitably collapses.

Warning Signs of a Crypto Ponzi

  • Guaranteed returns: Any platform promising fixed daily or weekly returns (e.g., "2% daily guaranteed") is almost certainly a Ponzi. Real investments fluctuate
  • Referral-heavy model: Excessive emphasis on recruiting new members with multi-tier referral bonuses is a hallmark of pyramid and Ponzi schemes
  • Opaque strategy: "Our proprietary AI trading algorithm" or "our team of expert traders" with no verifiable evidence. Legitimate funds show audited track records
  • Withdrawal difficulties: Increasing delays, minimum balance requirements, or "fees" to withdraw are signs the scheme is running low on incoming funds
  • Too good to be true: If a yield is dramatically higher than comparable opportunities (e.g., 100% APY when DeFi averages 3-8%), the excess yield is not sustainable

Historical Examples: BitConnect (2018, $2.5B lost), OneCoin ($4B+ lost), and countless smaller schemes follow identical patterns. The crypto changes, but the Ponzi playbook remains the same.

Romance and Social Engineering Scams

Also known as "pig butchering" scams, these are long-con operations where scammers build personal relationships (romantic or friendly) over weeks or months before introducing a "crypto investment opportunity." These scams have exploded in scale, with losses exceeding $3 billion in 2025 alone.

The Pig Butchering Playbook

  1. Initial contact: A stranger reaches out via dating apps, WhatsApp, LinkedIn, or social media with a friendly message
  2. Relationship building: Over days or weeks, they build rapport and trust, sharing personal stories and showing interest in your life
  3. The pivot: They casually mention their success in crypto trading and offer to "teach" you or show you their platform
  4. Small wins: You invest a small amount on their "recommended platform" and see quick profits (fabricated by the scam platform)
  5. Escalation: They encourage larger and larger investments, sometimes introducing urgency ("this opportunity closes tomorrow")
  6. The steal: When you try to withdraw, you are told you need to pay "taxes," "fees," or "insurance" first. These payments also disappear

NFT and Ordinal Scams

As the Bitcoin Ordinals ecosystem has grown, scammers have followed. Common NFT and Ordinal scams include:

  • Fake collections: Copycat projects mimicking popular Ordinal collections with slightly different names or art
  • Wash trading: Artificially inflating floor prices through self-dealing transactions to attract real buyers at inflated prices
  • Malicious minting sites: Fake mint pages that drain connected wallets instead of delivering inscriptions
  • Stolen art: Inscribing other artists' work and selling it as original Ordinals

Always verify Ordinal collections through official channels, check inscription numbers, and use trusted marketplaces with verification systems.

Universal Red Flags

Scam Warning Signs Checklist

  • Promises of guaranteed or unusually high returns
  • Pressure to invest quickly ("limited time offer")
  • Unsolicited contact from strangers about investment opportunities
  • Requests for your seed phrase or private keys
  • Celebrity endorsement claims (usually fake or unauthorized)
  • No verifiable company registration or team identity
  • Only positive reviews and testimonials (likely fabricated)
  • Withdrawal difficulties or unexpected "fees" to access your funds
  • Communication only through Telegram, Discord, or encrypted apps
  • Website domain registered recently (check WHOIS)
  • No smart contract audit from a recognized firm
  • Excessive referral bonuses and multi-level commission structures

How to Protect Yourself

Follow these security practices to minimize your risk of falling victim to crypto scams:

Wallet Security

  • Use a hardware wallet (Ledger, Trezor) for any significant holdings. Hardware wallets keep your private keys offline where malware cannot reach them
  • Never store your seed phrase digitally. Write it on metal or paper and store it securely offline
  • Use separate wallets for different purposes: a "hot" wallet for daily transactions with minimal funds, and a "cold" vault for long-term storage
  • Regularly revoke unnecessary token approvals using tools like Revoke.cash

Research Habits

  • Always verify URLs by typing them manually or using bookmarks. Never click links from emails, DMs, or social media posts
  • Research every project before investing: read the whitepaper, check the team, verify the smart contract, and read independent reviews
  • Use scam databases and reporting sites like scam.horse, scam.wiki, and scam.surf to check if a project has been reported
  • Follow blockchain security researchers on X for real-time scam alerts

Safe Practice: Before sending any crypto, always send a small test transaction first. Verify it arrives correctly before sending larger amounts. This simple habit has saved countless users from sending funds to wrong or malicious addresses.

What to Do If You Have Been Scammed

If you believe you have been the victim of a crypto scam, act quickly:

  1. Secure remaining assets: Immediately transfer any remaining funds in compromised wallets to a new, secure wallet. If you shared your seed phrase, assume the wallet is fully compromised
  2. Revoke token approvals: Use Revoke.cash to remove any smart contract permissions from compromised wallets
  3. Document everything: Save all communications, transaction hashes, wallet addresses, website URLs, and screenshots. This evidence is critical for reporting
  4. Report to authorities: File reports with the FBI's IC3 (ic3.gov), the FTC (reportfraud.ftc.gov), and your local law enforcement. For securities fraud, report to the SEC
  5. Report to the blockchain community: Submit scam reports to scam.horse and blockchain analysis platforms to help protect others
  6. Contact your exchange: If funds were sent from an exchange, contact their fraud department immediately. Some exchanges can freeze funds that have not been moved further
